IMCI — Integrated Management of Childhood Illness

WHO/UNICEF framework for children 0–5 years | Color-coded triage system

📌 Key Facts
  • Formulated by WHO + UNICEF (Philippines: + DOH)
  • Age groups: Newborn–2 months AND 2 months–5 years
  • Uses a limited number of clinical signs (sensitivity + specificity)
  • Leads to classification (not diagnosis) via color-coded triage
  • ALL sick children → check nutritional status, immunization, HIV (high-risk areas)

MAIN SYMPTOMS ASSESSED:

Older Children

  • Cough/difficult breathing
  • Diarrhea
  • Fever
  • Ear infection

Young Infants

  • Local bacterial infection
  • Diarrhea
  • Jaundice

Diarrhea — Classification & Management

WHO Definition: ≥3 liquid stools/day OR more watery than normal | Consistency > Frequency ⭐

Treatment Plans ⭐⭐⭐
📗 Plan A — Home Therapy

For: NO DEHYDRATION

4 Rules of Home Treatment:

  • 1. Extra Fluid
    <2 yrs: 50–100 mL after each loose stool
    ≥2 yrs: 100–200 mL after each loose stool
  • 2. Zinc Supplements
    2–6 months: ½ tablet (10 mg) daily × 14 days
    ≥6 months: 1 tablet (20 mg) daily × 14 days
  • 3. Continue Feeding
    Exclusive BF if <6 months
  • 4. When to Return
    (see return signs)
📙 Plan B — Clinic ORS

For: SOME DEHYDRATION

  • ORS over 4 hours in clinic
  • ORS (mL) = weight (kg) × 75 ⭐
  • <6 kg: 200–500 mL
  • 6–9 kg: 450–800 mL
  • 10–11 kg: 800–960 mL
  • 12–19 kg: 960–1600 mL
  • Reassess after 4 hrs → choose plan A/B/C
  • No food during 4-hr period (except BF)
📕 Plan C — IV Fluids

For: SEVERE DEHYDRATION

  • IV fluid: Ringer's Lactate (or Normal Saline)
  • Total: 100 mL/kg
  • Infants (<12 months):
    30 mL/kg in 1 hour → 70 mL/kg in 5 hours
  • Children (1–5 yrs):
    30 mL/kg in 30 mins → 70 mL/kg in 2.5 hours
  • *Repeat 30 mL/kg if radial pulse still weak
  • Reassess infant after 6 hrs, child after 3 hrs
  • Also give ORS ~5 mL/kg/hr when child can drink
Plan C Algorithm — "Can you give IV?"
1
YES: Can give IV immediately → Start IV (Ringer's Lactate 100 mL/kg) immediately. If child can drink → give ORS while drip is set up.

Vomiting in Children

Forceful expulsion of stomach contents — a coordinated reflex process | Not routinely treated

🧠 Vomiting Center of the Brain ⭐

Located in the area postrema (medulla) — Central Processing Unit consists of:

Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ) Dorsal Motor Vagal Nucleus Nucleus Tractus Solitarius
🔀 4 Pathways of Emetic Reflex ⭐⭐
PathwayMechanismExample
Mechanical Mechanoreceptors (bowel distension) + Chemoreceptors (irritants) in gut wall → vagal afferents Bowel obstruction, food poisoning
Bloodborne Toxins CTZ in area postrema responds to circulating neurotoxic agents Cisplatin (chemotherapy) ⭐
Motion Vestibular system (M₁, H₁ receptors) → mismatch in body position perception Motion sickness, car ride
Emotion Higher cortical pathway → response to anxiety, fear, strong smells Test anxiety, foul odors
⏱️ Types of Vomiting by Duration ⭐
ACUTE
Rapid onset (24–48 hrs)
Severe symptoms + dehydration
Examples: Gastroenteritis, food poisoning, bowel obstruction
CHRONIC
Several days to weeks
Low volume, infrequent
Examples: PUD, gallbladder disease, gastritis, food reaction
CYCLIC
Stereotypical + repetitive episodes
Sudden onset + asymptomatic intervals ⭐
Example: Cyclic vomiting syndrome
🔴 Red Flags for Vomiting ⭐⭐
  • Hematemesis (blood in vomit = UGIB)
  • Hematochezia (red/maroon stool = LGIB or massive UGIB)
  • Recurrent bilious emesis
  • Clinical dehydration
  • Evidence of shock
  • Focal neurologic changes
  • Abdominal distention
  • Absent or tympanitic bowel sounds
💊 Treatment Principles
  • Most children do NOT need treatment
  • If dehydrated → correct fluid + electrolyte abnormalities
  • Antiemetics/Prokinetics: NOT routinely given
  • Only used to minimize vomiting while investigating etiology
  • Side effects: sedation, may interfere with hydration
📋 Labs: Always check serum electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) in all cases of vomiting with dehydration

⭐ Reduced ORS advantages: ↓20% stool volume, ↓30% vomiting, ↓33% IV fluid use, promotes good water absorption

For acute non-cholera diarrhea (per 2024 WHO/UNICEF): Na 50–75 mEq/L | Glucose 75–90 mmol/L | Osmolarity 210–268 mOsm/L

✅ Suitable Fluids
  • ORS solution
  • Salted rice water
  • Salted yogurt drink
  • Vegetable or chicken soup with salt
  • Water in cereals (lugaw)
  • Plain water
  • Buko juice
  • Fresh, unsweetened juice
  • Weak, unsweetened tea

Give as much as child wants until diarrhea stops ⭐

❌ Unsuitable Fluids
  • Commercial carbonated beverages (Coke, Pepsi)
  • Commercial fruit juices
  • Sweetened tea
  • Coffee
  • Medicinal teas/infusions
⚠️ Sugary drinks → osmotic diarrhea + hypernatremia. Avoid drinks without salt AND sweetened drinks.

📌 Plays a central role in cellular growth + immune function ⭐

🍼 Diarrhea & Nutrition — Key Rules ⭐
  • Food should NEVER be withheld ⭐
  • Child's usual foods should NOT be diluted ⭐
  • Breastfeeding should ALWAYS be continued ⭐
  • Aim: Give as much nutrition-rich food as child will accept
  • Diet should be increased AFTER diarrhea stops

Other Therapies:

  • Antimicrobials: NOT routinely given. Only if bacterial cause (Shigella, Salmonella, E. coli, Clostridium)
  • Vitamin A: May be given in low-intake regions; important for immunity + epithelial lining
  • Antimotility drugs: DANGEROUS — causes severe paralytic ileus
  • Antiemetics: Causes sedation → fatal CNS toxicities
💉 Electrolyte Imbalance Reference
HYPOnatremia: <130 mmol/L HYPERnatremia: >150 mmol/L HypoKALEMIA: <3 mmol/L

ORS can adequately correct electrolyte imbalances in diarrhea when given appropriately.

🛡️ Prevention of Diarrhea
Safe drinking water Improved sanitation Handwashing with soap ⭐ Exclusive BF × 6 months Good food hygiene Rotavirus vaccine Measles vaccine Health education

Common Cold — Key Points
  • Infection limited to nasal epithelium
  • Most common cause: Rhinovirus
  • Young children: up to 8 bouts/year; adults: 3–6/year
  • Symptoms persist ≥10 days in kids (vs 5–7 days in adults)
  • Greenish phlegm ≠ bacterial infection (especially in infants)
  • Sinusitis NOT a differential if sinuses not yet developed (young infants)
  • IL-8 concentration = symptom severity marker
  • Bradykinin → sore throat and rhinitis
  • Self-limiting: viral shedding peaks 3–5 days post-infection

INEFFECTIVE / Not Given:

Cough suppressants Antibiotics Bronchodilators* Warm humidified air Echinacea High-dose Vitamin C

*except virus-induced reactive airway disease

Supportive Measures:

Oral hydration Nasal saline Paracetamol (fever/pain) Oxymetazoline (limited)

Bacterial Sinusitis ⭐

Sinus Development (E-M-S-F) ⭐

  • Ethmoid – Present at birth, pneumatized at birth
  • Maxillary – Present at birth, pneumatized at 4 years
  • Sphenoidal – Develops at 5 years
  • Frontal – Develops at 7–8 years

Viral vs Bacterial Sinusitis

  • Viral: 2–7 days, rhinorrhea + sneezing, self-limiting
  • Bacterial: >5–7 days, purulent discharge, facial pain/pressure, fever recurrence
  • Bacterial bugs: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis, S. aureus
  • Treatment: Amoxicillin 45 mg/kg/d × 10 days or 7 days after resolution
  • Complications: Periorbital cellulitis, orbital cellulitis (proptosis, ↓VA), intracranial infection
Pertussis (Whooping Cough) ⭐⭐

Causative agent: Bordetella pertussis (Gram-negative coccobacillus) | Transmission: droplet

PhaseDurationKey Features
Catarrhal 1–2 weeks Most infectious phase ⭐. Coryza, mild cough, sneezing, low fever. Similar to URTI.
Paroxysmal Weeks Staccato cough + inspiratory "whoop". Fever disappears. Worse at NIGHT. Post-tussive vomiting. Cyanosis, apnea, diaphoresis possible. Antibiotics NOT effective here.
Convalescent Weeks–months Residual cough triggered by irritants or another URI
💊 Treatment: Macrolides (Erythromycin 1st choice, Azithromycin, Clarithromycin)
Goal: ↓ carriage/spread, not stop infection
Minimal improvement if given in catarrhal phase
NOT effective in paroxysmal phase
⚠️ Lab clue: Leukemoid reaction (↑↑ WBC = severe infection)
Most dangerous to: Infants
Gold standard Dx: Nasopharyngeal culture; PCR also used
Immunity wanes 7–12 years post-vaccination/infection
GAS Pharyngitis ⭐

Clinical Features of GAS:

  • Rapid onset sore throat + fever
  • Red pharynx, enlarged tonsils, white/yellow exudates
  • Petechiae / doughnut lesions on soft palate
  • Strawberry tongue (white then red)
  • Enlarged, tender anterior cervical lymph nodes
  • Associated: headache, abdominal pain, vomiting, ear pain
  • NO cough, NO rhinorrhea (vs viral pharyngitis)
  • Scarlet fever: red papular rash starting from face → spreads

Management:

  • Gold standard Dx: Throat culture (blood agar)
  • RADT: very high specificity (>95%); if (+) no culture needed; if (−) confirm with culture
  • McIsaac score criteria (each = +1 pt): Fever >38°C, No cough, Tender anterior nodes, Tonsillar swelling/exudate, Age 3–14
  • 1st line: Penicillin/Amoxicillin × 10 days
  • Penicillin-allergic: Cephalosporins (mild) or Macrolides/Clindamycin (severe)
  • Goal: Prevent Acute Rheumatic Fever (if started ≤10 days)
  • NO effect on APSGN (post-streptococcal GN)

Lower Respiratory Tract Infections (LRTI)

Trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli | Pneumonia is the leading infectious cause of death in children <5 globally

🫁 LRTI Clinical Features → Diagnosis ⭐⭐⭐

Gradual onset, mild fever, wheezing, crackles, often in <2 years old Viral Pneumonia Alveoli affected. WBC normal or mildly ↑ (<20,000), lymphocyte predominance. No pleural effusion. No wheezing typically.

⭐ Viruses are the USUAL cause of LRTI in 1 month–5 year olds. RSV = most common cause of viral bronchiolitis and viral pneumonia.
Bronchiolitis ⭐⭐
  • Most common in infants; leading cause of morbidity/mortality in infants
  • Pathology: airway edema + ↑ mucus production + necrosis of airway epithelium → inflammation + edema + necrosis → bronchospasm
  • RSV = #1 cause (>90%)
  • PE: Diffuse wheezing, crackles, coarse prolonged expiratory phase, transmitted upper airway sounds
  • Wheezing may be heard without stethoscope
  • Routine chest X-ray: NOT recommended

NOT Routinely Recommended:

Bronchodilators Steroids Antibiotics Nebulizers (NSS) Antivirals
✅ Treatment: Supportive only. Assess hydration + oxygenation. RSV vaccine available in USA, not yet in Philippines.

Atypical Pneumonia ⭐
  • Organisms: Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, respiratory viruses
  • NOT identified with standard bacterial cultures or Gram stain
  • Common in school-aged children and adolescents
  • Causes up to 1/3 of CAP in children 5–15 yrs
  • Epidemics every 3–7 years
  • Peak: winter and early spring
  • Only ~10% present with high fever
  • CXR: diffuse/patchy interstitial infiltrates (worse than clinical signs)
  • Blood cultures: usually negative; WBC normal or mildly elevated
  • Consider if no response to beta-lactams
  • Treatment: Macrolides (Azithromycin preferred)
  • Bullous myringitis = rare but associated with M. pneumoniae
  • M. pneumoniae complications: hemolytic anemia, SJS, myocarditis, encephalitis
  • C. pneumoniae: associated with reactive airway disease
🚨 Respiratory Distress Assessment ⭐
🔴 First question to answer ALWAYS: Is the patient in respiratory distress or hypoxemic? Correct hypoxemia FIRST before taking history/PE. Position patient, oxygen support, ventilation.

Signs of Respiratory Distress:

Tachypnea Retractions (intercostal, subcostal, supraclavicular) Nasal flaring Grunting Cyanosis Paradoxical breathing

Approach to Respiratory Patient:

  • Step 1: Assess severity + correct hypoxemia
  • Step 2: Full history + PE (after stable)
  • Step 3: Organize differential diagnoses
  • Step 4: Diagnostic + management plan
  • Rule: LOCALIZE before you DIAGNOSE
🔴 Red Flag: Recurrent Pneumonia ⭐
⚠️ Consider ABNORMAL pulmonary events if: >2 episodes of pneumonia in a year OR >3 episodes of pneumonia overall → Requires further evaluation (immunodeficiency, anatomic abnormality, etc.)

Q1
A 4-month-old infant is diagnosed with SOME Dehydration. What is the correct daily zinc dosage?
  • A. 20 mg (1 tablet) daily × 14 days
  • B. 10 mg (½ tablet) daily × 14 days
  • C. 20 mg daily × 10 days
  • D. 10 mg daily × 7 days
Age 2–6 months → ½ tablet (10 mg) × 14 days. Children ≥6 months get 1 tablet (20 mg) × 14 days.
Q2
A child has SEVERE DEHYDRATION with no other severe classification. What is the most appropriate next step?
  • A. Plan B (ORS in clinic × 4 hours)
  • B. Refer urgently + ORS sips on the way
  • C. Plan C — IV fluids (Ringer's Lactate 100 mL/kg)
  • D. Ciprofloxacin for 3 days
Plan C = IV fluids for SEVERE DEHYDRATION with NO other severe classification. If there IS another severe classification → refer urgently with ORS sips on the way.
Q3
In Plan C for a 6-month-old infant, what is the IV fluid schedule?
  • A. 30 mL/kg in 30 minutes, then 70 mL/kg in 2.5 hours
  • B. 30 mL/kg in 1 hour, then 70 mL/kg in 5 hours
  • C. 50 mL/kg in 1 hour, then 50 mL/kg in 5 hours
  • D. 100 mL/kg in 6 hours
INFANTS (<12 months): 30 mL/kg in 1 hour → 70 mL/kg in 5 hours. CHILDREN (1–5 yrs): 30 mL/kg in 30 minutes → 70 mL/kg in 2.5 hours. Total = 100 mL/kg (Ringer's Lactate).
Q4
A 10-kg child with SOME dehydration needs Plan B ORS. How much ORS should be given in 4 hours?
  • A. 250 mL
  • B. 500 mL
  • C. 750 mL
  • D. 1000 mL
Formula: ORS (mL) = weight (kg) × 75. So 10 kg × 75 = 750 mL over 4 hours. ⭐
Q5
A child has blood in the stool with diarrhea. Which is the correct treatment?
  • A. Amoxicillin × 7 days, follow-up 5 days
  • B. Ciprofloxacin × 3 days, follow-up 3 days
  • C. Zinc × 14 days, follow-up 5 days
  • D. Plan B ORS × 4 hours, follow-up 3 days
Dysentery (blood in stool) = Ciprofloxacin × 3 days. Follow-up in 3 days (NOT 5). Common organisms: Shigella and Amoeba.
Q6
Which of the following is NOT one of the "4 Rules of Home Treatment" in Plan A?
  • A. Give extra fluid
  • B. Give Zinc supplements
  • C. Give Ciprofloxacin
  • D. Continue feeding
The 4 Rules of Plan A: (1) Give extra fluid, (2) Give Zinc supplements (2 months–5 years), (3) Continue feeding, (4) When to return. Ciprofloxacin is used for Dysentery, not standard home management.
Q7
What is the reduced osmolarity ORS composition (Na/Glucose/Osmolarity)?
  • A. Na 90 / Glucose 111 / Osmolarity 311
  • B. Na 75 / Glucose 111 / Osmolarity 268
  • C. Na 75 / Glucose 75 / Osmolarity 245
  • D. Na 50 / Glucose 60 / Osmolarity 210
Reduced Osmolarity ORS: Na 75 mEq/L, Glucose 75 mmol/L, Osmolarity 245 mOsm/L. Standard ORS: Na 90, Glucose 111, Osmolarity 311.
Q8
Which diarrhea mechanism is characterized by stool that persists even during fasting?
  • A. Osmotic diarrhea
  • B. Secretory diarrhea
  • C. Hypermotility
  • D. Mucosal invasion
Secretory diarrhea PERSISTS during fasting (normal osmolality). Osmotic diarrhea STOPS with fasting. Prototype for secretory = cholera.
Q9
A 3-year-old presents with sudden high fever, drooling, muffled voice, and tripod position. What should you do FIRST?
  • A. Have the child lie supine for examination
  • B. Start oral antibiotics immediately
  • C. Secure the airway — do NOT make child lie down
  • D. Give dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg IM
This is EPIGLOTTITIS — a medical emergency. ALWAYS secure the airway first. Do NOT ask child to lie down (can cause immediate respiratory arrest from anterior neck structures pressing on narrowed glottis). Treatment: Ceftriaxone IV × 10 days.
Q10
A 3-year-old has a barky cough, hoarseness, inspiratory stridor, and low-grade fever following a 3-day cold. What is the radiographic finding?
  • A. Thumbprint sign
  • B. Lobar consolidation
  • C. Steeple sign (subglottic narrowing)
  • D. Hyperinflation with bilateral infiltrates
Croup (LTB) = STEEPLE sign (subglottic narrowing on AP neck X-ray). Epiglottitis = THUMBPRINT sign. Bacterial pneumonia = Lobar consolidation. Viral pneumonia = hyperinflation + interstitial infiltrates.
Q11
Which vomiting pathway is responsible for cisplatin-induced nausea and vomiting?
  • A. Mechanical pathway
  • B. Bloodborne toxin pathway
  • C. Motion pathway
  • D. Emotional pathway
Cisplatin (chemotherapy) → circulating toxins → stimulate chemoreceptor trigger zone in area postrema → bloodborne toxin pathway. CTZ receptors: D₂, D₃, 5-HT₃, NK₁.
Q12
A 1-year-old with bronchiolitis is admitted. Which treatment is routinely given?
  • A. Oral steroids
  • B. Nebulized racemic epinephrine
  • C. Azithromycin
  • D. Supportive care (hydration + oxygenation assessment)
For bronchiolitis, NOT routinely given: bronchodilators, steroids, antibiotics, nebulizers (NSS), antivirals. Treatment = SUPPORTIVE only: hydration, oxygenation, monitoring.
Q13
An 8-year-old presents with fever, tonsillar erythema, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and absence of cough. What is the first-line antibiotic?
  • A. Azithromycin × 5 days
  • B. Amoxicillin × 10 days
  • C. Ceftriaxone IV × 10 days
  • D. Ciprofloxacin × 3 days
GAS pharyngitis (fever + tonsillar exudate + tender anterior cervical nodes + NO cough) → Amoxicillin × 10 days (preferred in children for taste + once-daily dosing). Goal: prevent acute rheumatic fever if started ≤10 days.
Q14
What is the most common organism causing bronchiolitis?
  • A. Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • B. Adenovirus
  • C. Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)
  • D. Parainfluenza virus
RSV causes >90% of bronchiolitis cases. RSV vaccine is approved in USA but not yet rolled out in the Philippines. RSV is also the most common cause of viral pneumonia in infants.
Q15
In the IMCI color-coded triage system, what does YELLOW signify?
  • A. Urgent hospital referral or admission
  • B. Most benign condition requiring home care only
  • C. Initiation of specific outpatient treatment
  • D. No further management needed
PINK = urgent referral. YELLOW = specific outpatient treatment (some danger signs). GREEN = supportive home care (benign).
Q16
Which of the following is the most infectious stage of pertussis?
  • A. Catarrhal stage
  • B. Paroxysmal stage
  • C. Convalescent stage
  • D. All stages are equally infectious
Catarrhal stage = MOST infectious phase (resembles common cold). Transmission can occur throughout the catarrhal phase and for 3 weeks after onset of paroxysmal phase. Antibiotics in paroxysmal phase = NOT effective.
Q17
A 10-year-old with a 2-week cough (no fever, minimal findings) is diagnosed with atypical pneumonia. Epidemics of this disease occur every:
  • A. 1–2 years
  • B. 2–4 years
  • C. 3–7 years
  • D. 10–15 years
Atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma/Chlamydia) epidemics occur every 3–7 years. It's common in school-aged children (5–15 yrs) and causes up to 1/3 of CAP in this age group.
Q18
In Plan B (ORS for Some Dehydration), which statement is TRUE?
  • A. Give ORS over 2 hours
  • B. Food should be given freely throughout the rehydration period
  • C. Breastfeeding must be stopped during Plan B
  • D. Food (except breastmilk) should not be given during the 4-hour rehydration period
During Plan B (4-hour ORS rehydration): Food (EXCEPT breastmilk) should NOT be given. Breastfeeding is ALWAYS continued. If Plan B continues >4 hours, then give food every 3–4 hours.
Q19
A 16-year-old presents with severe sore throat, difficulty opening her mouth, drooling, and "hot potato voice." The uvula is displaced to the right. Most likely diagnosis?
  • A. Retropharyngeal abscess
  • B. Peritonsillar abscess
  • C. Epiglottitis
  • D. Croup
Peritonsillar abscess: asymmetric tonsillar bulge + uvula deviation + hot potato voice + trismus. More common in OLDER children/adolescents. GAS + oropharyngeal anaerobes. Retropharyngeal abscess = bulge BEHIND uvula + neck stiffness + younger children (<6 yrs).
Q20
Which diarrhea pathogen is associated with BLOODY stools and WBCs in stool?
  • A. Rotavirus
  • B. Vibrio cholerae
  • C. Shigella
  • D. Cryptosporidium
Mucosal invasion mechanism → bloody stools + WBCs in stool. Pathogens: Salmonella, Shigella (dysentery), Amebiasis, Yersinia, Campylobacter. Cholera/Rotavirus = secretory/osmotic = watery, non-bloody.
Q21
Which of the following statements about viral pneumonia vs. bacterial pneumonia is CORRECT?
  • A. Viral pneumonia commonly presents with lobar consolidation
  • B. Pleural effusion is more common in viral pneumonia
  • C. Viral pneumonia WBC is usually <20,000/mm³ with lymphocyte predominance
  • D. Wheezing is more common in viral pneumonia
Viral pneumonia: WBC normal or mildly elevated (<20,000), lymphocyte-predominant. X-ray: hyperinflation, bilateral interstitial infiltrates, peribronchial cuffing. Wheeze and pleural effusion are more common in BACTERIAL pneumonia.
Q22
In the IMCI algorithm, diarrhea lasting ≥14 days WITH dehydration present is classified as?
  • A. Persistent Diarrhea
  • B. Severe Persistent Diarrhea
  • C. Dysentery
  • D. Severe Dehydration
Persistent Diarrhea (≥14 days): WITH dehydration = Severe Persistent Diarrhea → treat dehydration (Plan C) → refer to hospital. WITHOUT dehydration = Persistent Diarrhea → multivitamins/minerals/zinc × 14 days + Vitamin A + follow-up 5 days.
Q23
For a child under 2 years old with Plan A, how much extra ORS should be given after each loose stool?
  • A. 25–50 mL
  • B. 50–100 mL
  • C. 100–200 mL
  • D. 200–400 mL
Plan A additional fluid: ≤2 years = 50–100 mL after each loose stool. ≥2 years = 100–200 mL after each loose stool.
Q24
A 12-year-old with known allergic rhinitis and asthma develops fever 39°C, headache, purulent nasal discharge, and facial tenderness 10 days after a cold. The CORRECT antibiotic treatment is?
  • A. Amoxicillin 45 mg/kg/day BID × 10 days
  • B. Ciprofloxacin × 5 days
  • C. Azithromycin × 3 days
  • D. No antibiotics — supportive care only
Acute bacterial sinusitis (recurrent fever + purulent discharge >10 days + facial pain) → Amoxicillin 45 mg/kg/day BID/TID × 10 days (or 7 days after symptom resolution) is the antibiotic of choice.
Q25
What is the recommended initial treatment for MODERATE croup?
  • A. Ceftriaxone IV × 10 days
  • B. Supportive care only; most cases self-limited
  • C. Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg + nebulized racemic epinephrine if stridor at rest
  • D. Amoxicillin × 7 days
Moderate/Severe croup: Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg (single dose oral/IM) = MAINSTAY. Add nebulized racemic epinephrine if stridor at rest persists. Dexamethasone reduces severity, duration, and need for hospitalization/intubation. Croup is viral → no antibiotics.
