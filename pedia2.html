⚠️ Abusive Head Trauma (AHT)
Classic Triad: Subdural hematoma + Retinal hemorrhage + Fractures (rib & metaphyseal)
  • Mechanism: shaking, direct impact, asphyxia, or combination
  • Retinal hemorrhage = most common ophthalmic finding; multilayer, extending to periphery
  • Appears BEFORE coma
  • Children may have no external signs of trauma
  • Imaging: CT/MRI — best 5–7 days after injury
Shaken Baby Syndrome: Infants <3 y/o; acceleration-deceleration forces; 10% of child abuse cases; mortality up to 25%

Rule: Bruises over bony prominences (knees, elbows) = accidental. Bruises on chest, back, in patterns = abuse.
🔴 High-Risk Children
  • Age <4 years or adolescents
  • Unwanted children; fail parental expectations
  • Special needs, persistent crying
  • Abnormal physical features

⚖️ Child Abuse vs. Neglect — Key Distinction

🔴 Child ABUSE = Commission
TypeExamples
PhysicalBeating, shaking, burning, biting
Verbal/PsychologicalFoul language, name-calling, labeling → depression, poor self-esteem, lack of empathy
SexualAny unwanted sexual behavior; usually by known caregiver
🔵 Child NEGLECT = Omission
  • Failure to meet basic needs: food, shelter, healthcare, education
  • Child not in school = neglect (education is a RIGHT)
  • Parent absent → child neglected
Remember: Abuse = doing something harmful. Neglect = failing to do something necessary.

🔔 Sexual Abuse — High-Yield Points

  • Usually by caregiver or close family member — someone known!
  • Always screen for this type
  • Preschool/school-age: compulsive masturbation, attempting sex acts = alert
  • Teens: promiscuity, prostitution, running away, unwanted pregnancy
Long-term adult outcomes of abuse:
Depression · Smoking · Obesity · High-risk sexual behavior · Alcohol/drug abuse · Perpetuation of abuse

📋 Abuse Reporting Algorithm

🚨 Abuse Suspected → Report to CPU (Child Protection Unit) + Police
▼
❓ Did abuse occur within 72 hours?
✅ YES (<72 hrs):
Send to referral center for immediate exam + forensic evidence collection
⚠️ NO (>72 hrs):
Check for acute signs/symptoms
→ If YES: refer immediately
→ If NO: non-acute exam by expert
Always: Treat medical problems FIRST → Ensure safety → Document (photos, verbatim quotes) → Report

🔪 Bariatric Surgery Indications

  • BMI ≥35 kg/m² (or ≥120% of 95th) + serious comorbidities (T2DM, sleep apnea, pseudotumor cerebri, severe NAFLD)
  • BMI ≥40 kg/m² (or ≥140% of 95th)
  • After failing 6 months of multidisciplinary weight management
Most common procedures in teens:
• Laparoscopic Roux-en-Y gastric bypass
• Vertical sleeve gastrectomy

Nutritional complications: Lifelong supplementation of vitamins A, B1, B2, B6, B12, D, E, K + copper, iron

🏥 Treatment of Malnutrition

  • Start at 20% of child's present intake — never force feed
  • Gradually increase to 50–75% of normal energy requirement
  • Then increase by 10–20% per day once started
  • Catch-up growth: weight >50th percentile + increased protein + vitamins/minerals
Refeeding Syndrome: Fluid retention, hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia
Kwashiorkor: DO NOT give iron during acute rehab — high ferritin → oxidative stress

🌡️ Definitions

Fever = Rectal temp ≥38°C (100.4°F)
Category°C°F
Normal37–3998.6–100.4
Low Grade38.1–39100.5–102.2
Moderate Grade39.1–40102.3–104.0
High Grade40.1–41.1104.1–106

🌡️ Temperature Measurement

MethodValue
RectalUsually HIGHEST
AxillaryUsually LOWEST
TympanicBest reflects CORE temperature (shared blood supply with hypothalamus)
Cerumen ↓ tympanic temp (obstructs radiation). Tachypnea ↓ oral temp ~0.5°C per 10 breaths/min increase. Hemiparesis ↓ axillary temp 0.5°C on weak side.

🔬 Fever Mechanism

Exogenous pyrogens (bacteria, viruses, toxins, vancomycin, amphotericin B, allopurinol) — do NOT directly cause fever
▼ stimulate immune cells
Endogenous pyrogens released:
IL-1, IL-6, TNF (from macrophages, neutrophils, lymphocytes)
▼ act on hypothalamus
↑ Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) — directly resets hypothalamic thermostat
▼
↑ Set point → FEVER
Body activates: shivering + vasoconstriction
Why fever spikes at night: Cortisol ↓ at night → immune system more active → ↑ cytokines → ↑ set point. NOT a sign of worsening!

💉 Drug Fever — Key Points

  • Caused by medication without underlying infection
  • Fever usually sustained, may exceed 40.5°C
  • Patient may appear well despite high fever (mismatch = clue!)
  • Diagnosis of exclusion
  • Common drugs: Allopurinol, Vancomycin, Amphotericin B, anti-seizure drugs
Discontinue drug → fever resolves in 48–72 hours
(except iodide: may take ≥1 month)
Malignant Hyperthermia: Rare genetic disorder; pseudocholinesterase deficiency; triggered by halothane, desflurane, succinylcholine; treat with Dantrolene

🔬 Serious Bacterial Infection (SBI)

Occurs in 7–13% of neonates/young children
CauseFrequency
UTI5–13% (most common)
Bacteremia1–2%
Meningitis0.2–0.5% (least common but most severe)

Most common bacteria: E. coli → Group B Streptococcus
Consider HSV in children <28 days!

Septic Workup
  • CBC (infection markers)
  • Blood culture + sensitivity
  • Urinalysis + urine culture
  • CSF analysis
  • Chest X-ray (if needed)
  • HSV testing
  • Enterovirus PCR
  • Procalcitonin, CRP

💊 Management of Fever

Fever is adaptive — treat mainly for comfort, NOT to cure the cause. Antipyretics do NOT change disease course.
DrugDoseFrequencyKey Note
Paracetamol10–15 mg/kg/doseq4h; max 5 dosesHepatotoxic; widely accessible
Ibuprofen5–10 mg/kg/doseq8hGood for poor appetite; GI bleeding risk; DON'T give without knowing cause
Fever >41°C could indicate CNS hemorrhage/hypothalamic disorder — MUST TREAT IMMEDIATELY; antipyretics alone won't reverse this!

SBI Empiric Antibiotic Treatment (Neonates)

  • 4th-generation cephalosporin (Cefepime) — most common
  • OR 3rd-gen cephalosporin + ampicillin
  • OR aminoglycoside + ampicillin

❓ FWS vs. FUO

FeatureFever Without Source (FWS)Fever of Unknown Origin (FUO)
Duration<7 days≥8 days
Temperature≥38°C≥38°C documented by HCP
CauseNot yet identifiedCannot be identified despite evaluation
Key pointCan progress to FUODiagnostic dilemma; usually uncommon presentation of common disease
Etiology of FUO: Infectious (50%, most common) > Rheumatologic/CTD > Malignancy > Drug fever > Factitious > Inflammatory > Neurologic
Pseudo-FUO: Multiple benign self-limited infections parents perceive as one episode. Tool: Fever diary!
Prognosis: Children with FUO have BETTER prognosis than adults. 25% never get a diagnosis; fever often abates spontaneously.
